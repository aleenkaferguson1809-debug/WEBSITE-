The Process

From hand to bin in eight steps

Every sort runs through three connected systems — a website doing the seeing and thinking, a microbit doing the moving, and a second microbit doing the counting. Here's the full chain, in the order it actually fires.

01 📷
Camera scans the object
A live feed captures the item the moment it's placed in the entry point.
02 🧠
AI classifies it
A Teachable Machine model decides: plastic bottle, or chip packet — with a live confidence score.
03 🌐
Website confirms the result
The result has to hold steady above a confidence threshold first, so a flickering guess can't trigger the sort.
04 🔌
Command sent over USB
The GitHub-hosted site sends "BOTTLE" or "CHIPS" to microbit 1 via Web Serial.
05 ⚙️
Servo swings the paddle
microbit 1 drives a GeekServo motor, angling the paddle toward the correct chute.
06 ↩️
Paddle resets to centre
Gravity does the rest, dropping the item into its bin while the paddle re-centres for the next one.
07 📡
Wireless message fires
microbit 1 radios "RECYCLE" or "RUBBISH" to microbit 2 — no wires between the two units.
08 🔢
Counter ticks up
microbit 2's four-digit display increases the running total — a live usage record for the school.
Under the Hood

Three connected devices, one decision

The system is really three small computers talking to each other over three different connections — USB serial, microbit radio, and the chute itself. No single part does everything; each one hands off to the next.

DEVICE 0

The Website (GitHub Pages)

Hosts the Teachable Machine AI model and shows the camera feed, the predicted class, and the live confidence percentage as it scans.

  • Holds a result steady before accepting it, to avoid reacting to a flickering guess
  • Sends the final decision to microbit 1 over a USB Web Serial connection
USB SERIAL → "BOTTLE" / "CHIPS"
DEVICE 1

microbit + Boson board + GeekServo

The main sorting controller. Receives the command, drives the servo to the matching side of the chute, then returns it to centre.

  • Bottle → moves toward the recycling chute
  • Chips → moves toward the general-rubbish chute
  • One small motor steering a paddle — gravity does the lifting, not the servo
MICROBIT RADIO → "RECYCLE" / "RUBBISH"
DEVICE 2

microbit + Grove Shield + 4-digit display

The sustainability dashboard. Every successful sort received over radio adds one to the running total, shown live on the display.

  • No wired connection to the sorting unit — fully wireless handoff
  • Turns sorting events into a measurable usage record for the school

One entry point, one sloped chute, one paddle deciding the side. The mechanical layout — schematic, prototype build still in progress with microbits and breadboard wiring.

Why This Isn't Just a Sign on a Bin

Most "smart bins" stop at a label.
This one acts.

A lot of school sustainability projects identify waste and display an instruction. This system is the rarer kind: it makes the decision, executes it mechanically, and logs the outcome — without a person needing to read or trust a label at all.

CREATIVITY

A pinball machine, repurposed

The sorting mechanism borrows directly from pinball paddles: gravity moves the object, and a small servo only has to nudge a lightweight divider — not lift or carry the item's full weight.

AI UNDERSTANDING

Confidence over guesswork

The AI model (Google Teachable Machine) doesn't get to act on a single lucky frame — its prediction must stay above a confidence threshold for a sustained period before the website trusts it enough to move the servo.

DIFFICULTY

Three systems, two protocols

A browser-based AI model, two microbits, and a servo all have to agree with each other across two different communication protocols — USB Web Serial and microbit radio — to complete one sort.

IMPACT

Turns one bin into a data point

The four-digit counter means the bin doesn't just sort waste — it produces a usage number a school can actually track over a term, turning a one-off invention into ongoing sustainability data.
